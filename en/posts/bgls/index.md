# The Berglas Effect



> I cannot stress enough that the moment, and the experience, are far more important than any method.
> 
> ——DAVID BERGLAS

## The Berglas Effect

Magic looks like a miracle because what it shows seems to go beyond the ordinary rules of reality. Most people think of magic as something grand and unbelievable: flying through the air, escaping from impossible traps, pulling things out of nowhere, making a person appear in an instant. The Berglas Effect, by contrast, is far plainer on stage[^1]:

- Imagine a magician taking out a deck of cards, letting people inspect it to confirm that nothing has been tampered with, and then **never touching the deck again**.
- Next, the magician randomly picks spectator A and asks them to think of any suit and any value.
- Then the magician asks spectator B to shuffle the deck and name a number at random.
- Spectator B turns over the cards one by one in order, and the card at that exact number is the very card spectator A only thought of.

The appeal of the Berglas Effect is that, in terms of sheer procedure, it makes the audience feel the trick should be impossible:

- No stooges. The spectators are chosen at random, and the crucial choices are random as well.
- No sleight of hand. The magician does not touch the deck.
- No gimmicks. The deck is openly inspected.
- The logical shock: two random choices somehow converge into an event with vanishingly small odds. It feels like a miracle.

> We will teach you how to use your personality instead of your hands; your charm instead of your sweat; your mind instead of your fingers.
> 
> ——*The Berglas Effect*

## The real appeal: method and performance


![Cover of *The Berglas Effect*](/img/巴格拉斯效果.zh-cn-1777289062480.webp)


Before getting to the explanations in this book, I have to pass on one core idea from it: **performing magic has a deeper appeal than exposing how the trick works**.

Sleights and props can certainly produce the outward form of a magic effect, but the way those sleights and props are used belongs to performance. Once the method is exposed, people quickly lose interest. What holds the mystery is the performance that makes people believe **the magician has done nothing at all**. That is where the endless detail lies. Even in a scientific age, how do you lead people to believe that a miracle has happened here and now? There is a kind of philosophy inside performance and presentation, and that is where the charm of the magician 🎩 comes from.

Once we start paying attention to the essence of performance, the techniques of magic stop being limited to magic alone. A matador raising a <font color="#ff0000">bright red cape</font> high in the air shows the same thing: exaggerated movement and the choice of color are both indispensable to the bullfight[^2]!

## How the Berglas Effect is performed

The first secret is that the Berglas Effect is not a single performance. It is a whole series of performances.

The principles of the Berglas Effect are:

1. There is no card force: no making the spectator choose a specific card through suggestion, sleight of hand, or similar techniques.
2. It does not depend on psychological fishing, such as asking the spectator to name a favorite color or number.
3. During the performance, the magician is not allowed to walk around holding a prepared deck. The deck needs to stay on the table or out of sight.
4. The magician is not allowed to use their hands to locate the card in the deck, and the count cannot simply run backward.
5. The magician is not allowed to use secret maneuvers to control the card into the required position.

In fact, no single performance fully satisfies all five conditions. Sometimes David only meets conditions 1-4; sometimes he only meets 2-5. But if you do it often enough, what people remember is the impression that all of them were satisfied.

For example, once the spectator has named the card, the magician only then starts thinking about how to present the Berglas Effect. But the performance has to make the audience believe that the Berglas Effect was already in place before the card was ever named.

One technique is that David really does touch the deck in some performances, but spectators are likely to forget that later when they recall what happened. In magic, every action produces a result, and one result can cover another, or a large action can bury a smaller one[^3].

That is why David keeps stressing this point: in magic, the moment and the experience matter far more than any method. What matters about the Berglas Effect is not reproducing it under every possible condition, but discovering the conditions in which the Berglas Effect can appear.


In fact, the magician has memorized the deck. So when the spectator names a number, the magician already knows where the target card is in the stack. What remains is figuring out how to make the card appear there.

![This means the magician can quickly track a card's relative position from the top, the bottom, or the middle](/img/巴格拉斯效果.zh-cn-1777296738831.webp)


When it comes to the number-selection stage, the magician may first frame it like this: choose a number below 10, around 10, between 10 and 20, or above 20. **The moment the magician starts giving examples, they are already nudging the spectator's choice** [^4]. Once the spectator has chosen a number, there are still ways to handle it: directly cut off that number of cards, count from the bottom upward, or count from the top downward. By using the display after the spectator cuts the deck (the spectator may expose the position of the bottom card), the magician can also work out the rough relative positions of all the cards.

For example, if the magician knows the needed position is 8, they might say, "Give me an example, 5, 6, 7, something like that." That is really a way of suggesting a small number, and it works even more strongly than simply saying, "Give me a small number." Tone, cadence, and pauses can all act as suggestion. **The most important part of the performance is making the spectator feel they are choosing freely**.

Doing all of the above quietly and naturally is the hardest part of magic performance. The magician has to judge, at the very least, before which spectator action they must finish their thinking. One useful advantage is this: **the spectators do not actually know what the magician's next step is**.

So if the spectator cuts the deck and you miss the key card, you can simply let them keep cutting until you recognize the locator card. **From the magician's point of view, this is just making the trick easier; from the spectator's point of view, the extra cutting only makes the effect look more complicated**.

Another technique lies in how the number is interpreted. Suppose the magician knows the correct card is the fifth card, but the spectator casually says 4. The magician can explain it this way: remove the first 4 cards, and the fifth card is the one you are thinking of.

Following the same logic, the magician can ask the spectator to count from the middle. If the spectator says 3, for instance, the magician can first ask them to cut to the middle, and then count 3 from the top or from the bottom.

If there is a Joker in the deck, the count can also be based on the Joker's relative position. More flexibly still, it can be based on some other card, or on excluding a certain category of cards. If the spectator says 13, for example, the magician might say: do not count the black cards, only count the red ones, and then count in order. Of course, that makes the counting itself more complicated.

> In other words, for the same target card, there can be many different numbers that reach it: counting from the top, from the bottom, from the middle, relative to a Joker, and so on... (David says that for an ordinary card, he usually has more than ten different numbers available.)

The next technique is switching people. If one person names a number that does not help you, you can use that number to change spectators. For example: you chose 3? Good. Then the next spectator will be the third person to your left, and they can name a new number (or a new card). Asking the spectator whether they want to change their number works on the same principle.

> These methods work in two directions at once: they are low-key, natural ways of controlling the situation, and they also highlight that the magician never touched the cards.


Other actions work the same way. Naturalness is the hardest part. Magic performance is never about announcing to the audience what you are about to do; that feels unnatural. Every move has to take into account what the audience noticed and what kind of information they think they received.

## Liu Qian's version

There are currently four recorded versions of Liu Qian performing it on video.

[Liu Qian's four performances of the Berglas Effect [full HD collection]](https://www.bilibili.com/video/BV12b4y1R7zf/?spm_id_from=333.337.search-card.all.click&vd_source=0e137afa81fa006aaa9f39b597699a06)

Also recommended: Liu Qian's interview with Luo Yonghao: [Full interview | Magic master Liu Qian × Luo Yonghao! The Liu Qian you may not know](https://www.bilibili.com/video/BV1dqkZBhEz3/?spm_id_from=333.337.search-card.all.click&vd_source=0e137afa81fa006aaa9f39b597699a06)

- Compared with David's version of the Berglas Effect, what feels most frightening is how mature Liu Qian's later two performances are[^5]. He always turns over the cards from the top downward, which means there is no overexplaining of the number. In Liu Qian's version, there are only two people: one names the suit and value, the other names the number, and that alone creates the double-random structure. (**It almost feels as if true freedom under any conditions has really been achieved!**)
- During the actual performance phase, Liu Qian really does not touch the cards.
- The decks are not the same. I actually compared the suit order in two of the decks, and the relative order is different.


> There is no shuffling or cutting at all, so it is reasonable to guess that Liu Qian has memorized the deck. If there are no stooges, then the key must lie either in the card-selection stage or in the props.
